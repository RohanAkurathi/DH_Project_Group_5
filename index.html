Deportation Data Analysis

Diving beyond the numbers

Immigration and deportation policy has become a topic of fierce contention within the past few years in the United States. Under the current federal administration, the United States has seen a sharp increase in deportations, with some considering them favorable and others harshly criticizing these policies. However, these policies are not new to the United States; changing deportation policies dates back centuries. Our study looks at the history of these deportations, specifically within the years 1900 to 1921, highlighting the impact they had on different communities in the U.S. The national and racial categories with the highest raw amounts of deportations were the English and Mexican, with English communities mainly residing in the Northeast and Mexican communities residing in the Southwest.

Our study draws upon numerical data regarding the number of deportations and their reasons, peer-reviewed studies of their impact, congressional hearings regarding legislation, and different policies that impacted both immigration and deportations. These sources provide important context for the social and political climate of the early 20th century to better understand why these deportations occurred, and more importantly, how both the English and Mexican communities within the U.S. were affected.

Human Impact

‚ÄúYou can't undo a deportation.‚Äù - Author Attorney Jonathan Shapiro. Between 1900 and 1921, the United States deported thousands of Mexicans and English from their communities. These actions by federal agencies showed a growing sentiment and national effort to control immigration. The government often justifies these actions by citing public concern about economic, social, and welfare issues. These actions were often driven by perceived threats rather than by substantive factual evidence. Deportation policies became incredibly targeted and systematic, going after specific groups such as Mexicans and the English. Within these communities, specifically these deportations created long-lasting effects in millions of people's daily lives; disrupting families, creating feelings of instability, reshaping the workforce, and creating a sense of vulnerability. People lived with the knowledge that any interaction with officials could result in separation from their homes and loved ones. Going beyond the pure numbers and looking at the historical context of this time period through various forms of media allows us to understand how immigration policy uproots and shapes entire communities. The gap between policy intentions and human consequences reveals a crucial hole in how we understand this period of American history.

Policy Shift

The period of deportations between 1900 and 1921 saw a more modernization of immigration enforcement within the United States. These deportations were characterized by the establishment of ‚Äúdeportation arrests‚Äù (Nash), allowing enforcing agencies to target non-citizens based on their status. This was different than before, as most were deported for criminal activity. Immigrants were less protected legally against mass removal compared to criminal charges (Gulasekaram). Much of this policy change was due to fears in the American public, many of which surrounded politics and economic conditions (Seller). The fears surrounding economic conditions were devised from concerns that immigration had led to slowed wages of U.S.-born citizens and older immigrant workers (Lauck), leading Congress to enact ‚Äúrestrictive and selective‚Äù measures aimed at controlling the ‚Äúquality‚Äù of immigrants that entered the U.S. (Peirce). As time passed, from 1908 onward, there was a shift in deportation reasoning to paperwork violations instead of criminal issues (King); however, wartime anxieties also led to new proposals such as the 1919 Bill (H.R. 13965) that aimed to deport ‚Äúundesired aliens‚Äù for ‚Äúany offense‚Äù (Deportation of Interned Aliens). This system was criticized for breaking apart families (Kellor), which is a large factor that harmed many communities like the English and Mexicans within the U.S. Much of these deportations were due to racial discrimination and happened in communities along the southwestern border, leading to a ‚Äúhistorical memory‚Äù of vulnerability and a distrust of authorities (Hernandez). Despite all of this, there are some contradictions that exist between the reasoning for deportations. In this period, policy was aimed at reducing economic stress on U.S.-born citizens through slowed immigration and increased deportations; however, many scholars disagree and argue that this change was due to racial discrimination that was heavily prevalent in this period. Although scholars may disagree on the reasoning for the deportations, what can be agreed upon is that they had a significant impact on communities, such as Mexican and English, for better or for worse. Much of what has not been researched surrounding this topic is the impact it has had on the mental health of people within these communities. There are plenty of economic and population data, yet the impact it had on development and its connection to humanistic data may remain unknown.

Project Lens

The significance of our project lies in its ability to connect historical deportation patterns to broader questions about how immigration policy shapes the lived experiences of communities. While scholars have written extensively about the political and economic motivations behind deportations, far less attention has been given to how these policies affected people on a human level‚Äîparticularly their mental health, family stability, and long-term sense of security. I am working on this topic because I want to find out how the rise of deportation enforcement between 1900 and 1921 changed the daily lives of immigrant communities so that I can help others understand the deeper social and emotional impact behind these historical events. Our data visualizations reveal measurable patterns‚Äîsuch as increases in arrest rates and policy shifts toward ‚Äúquality control‚Äù‚Äîthat can be linked with meaningful human consequences. By examining deportation through both statistical trends and humanistic perspectives, this project encourages readers to think beyond policy numbers and recognize the lasting psychological and social effects these actions left on Mexican, English, and other immigrant groups. Ultimately, the significance of our work is its effort to bridge quantitative historical data with the lived experiences that are often missing from traditional immigration research.

Synthesis

Conclusions

Impact on Immigrant Communities

Deportations between 1900 and 1921 altered immigrant communities by establishing an environment of uncertainty, social pressure, and institutional scrutiny that reached well beyond the persons actually taken. Deportation enforcement tended to concentrate on administrative infractions or changes in federal policy for groups like English immigrants, so communities saw the system as a bureaucratic annoyance rather than an existential danger to their presence in the nation. In contrast, Mexican communities endured a deeper and more constant sense of vulnerability. Rising removals during the Mexican Revolution, coupled with increased federal authority through legislation like the Immigration Act of 1907 and later wartime measures, meant Mexican migrants increasingly lived with the potential of unexpected expulsion. In order to evade authorities' attention, some migrants were encouraged to migrate seasonally or congregate in supportive enclaves as a result of these policies. Across both groups, deportation policies suggested that membership in the United States was dependent on political moods, economic situations, and altering concepts of national identity, emphasizing that immigrant life in this period was molded as much by changeable enforcement as by opportunity.

Laws and Regulations Affecting Deportation

Laws and restrictions enacted between 1892 and 1921 profoundly transformed how the United States targeted and expelled immigrants from different backgrounds, causing disparate results across nations. The federal government's power to exclude and deport people for economic, moral, and medical reasons was established by early laws such the Immigration Act of 1907 and the Expatriation Act, but these criteria were used inconsistently based on racial presumptions and public opinion. Due to administrative or technical problems, such as becoming a public charge within a year of arrival, English immigrants‚Äîwho were often thought to be culturally compatible‚Äîwere more likely to be deported. By contrast, immigrants from Mexico and other non-European regions faced greater scrutiny as new legislation broadened the definition of deportable acts. The Anarchist Exclusion Act added categories such as anarchists and those with certain disabilities, and the Immigration Act of 1907 broadened the federal government‚Äôs ability to deport individuals for moral and medical reasons while increasing funding for enforcement. Mexican migrants, already moving north in response to political instability and economic pressures, encountered a system that now had both the legal tools and the institutional capacity to target them more aggressively. Together, these regulations created a deportation regime that was formally national in scope but substantively shaped by racial hierarchies and evolving ideas about who belonged in the United States.

Reflection of Political Beliefs

Deportation rates from 1892 to 1921 reflect the political beliefs that defined the United States during this period. It was able to reveal how immigration enforcement served as both a policy tool and a reflection of national identity debates. The rise in deportations in this period aligns with the expanding federal bureaucracy that viewed immigration as more and more of an issue that required centralized control, particularly as new laws broadened the categories of who could be removed. Periods of increasing removals often correlated with increased surges in nativist sentiment, economic downturns, and fears about social disorder. By the late 1910s and early 1920s, wartime nationalism and a rising commitment to ‚ÄúAmericanization‚Äù produced stricter standards in who the government let into the country. These patterns show that deportation practices were not neutral administrative actions but were shaped by shifting public attitudes toward race and the meaning of American identity.

Data Overview

The information that is included is mainly the number of deportations from the United States in the period from 1892 to 1921. Additionally, the dataset provides more information, such as the ethnicities and nationalities of the people who were deported. The locational data includes what state and city each person was deported from, and what country they were deported to. The reasoning for why each person was deported is also included, which ranges from disease to some people committing crimes. One piece of information that might be interesting to look into is people being deported for being considered an ‚Äúidiot/imbecile.‚Äù We are unsure as to what this constitutes, as it seems like a very subjective reason to deport someone. Another thing that this data reveals is that a large number of Germans and Italians were deported, despite the Chinese Exclusion Act being in place. It appears as though these populations were heavily targeted. Overall, this data provides a lot of background information that will help guide us as we conduct our research and helps reveal what communities may have been targeted.

What Information Is Included & What This Dataset Can Illuminate

Although this data reveals much valuable information regarding all forty thousand plus deportations in America from 1893‚Äì1921, it does not reveal the personal stories of the people listed, as well as the contexts that they were deported under. Political pressure, racial problems, or economic hardship could all be reasons why these decisions were made. It also lacks contextual information regarding what the United States' deportation policy was like at the time, as well as what leaders were in power that may have influenced an influx of deportations. It also does not represent the human aspect at all, and what effect these displacements have had on the families and their lives. This is the main problem with quantitative data, as it lacks context that explains the deeper political, social, and racial implications of what affected these numbers.

How the Data Was Generated & Origins & Funding

The data is generated onto a sheet. Most of the data centers on the port of deportation, the grounds for deportation, and the destination of deportation. Some columns will also depict exact longitude and latitudes for where the deportation took place. The source itself was found through the Harvard Dataverse Website. However, the author of the dataset is Torrie Hester, a history professor at Saint Louis University. She seems to be very involved with educating about deportation, and this dataset was made for ‚ÄúDeportations from the United States: A Visual History‚Äù (DVH) (2020-11-10). This data was published on November 23rd, 2021; however, it was added to the repository on November 10th, 2020. There is no public confirmation on who funded this data.

What Information Is Left Out (and Cannot Reveal)

A lot of information is left out about this dataset. This dataset is sourced from the Harvard Dataverse website, which is accessible on Canvas; although the website has the metadata section, this only describes the general contexts of the dataset and does not explain the meaning of the categories and values of the dataset. Additionally, this dataset contains numerous blank spaces, mostly in the DeportedFromLat, DeportedFromLong, DeportedTo, DeportedToName, and DeportedToiso_alpha categories, which provide information on the port of deportation and the destination of deportation‚Äîdata important for understanding deportation routes and patterns; some data in NatandracName and Natandraciso_alpha are also missing, but compared to the amount of missing data in the previously mentioned categories, it's just a small amount.

The dataset also does not provide any personal information about the deported people, such as their family backgrounds, education levels, or economic status, which are essential clues for understanding the reasons for deportation and the deportation patterns. Moreover, the reason for this missing data is not explicit. This also ties into what the dataset cannot reveal: without contextual, personal, and legal details, we cannot see individual experiences, the agencies driving enforcement, or the full legal landscape behind each removal.

Ideological Effects

The dataset shows certain ideological effects in how deportation data was recorded and presented. It treats people mainly as numbers and cases instead of individuals with personal stories. This reflects how the U.S. government at that time viewed deportation‚Äîas an administrative or legal issue rather than a human one. The way the data is organized also suggests a belief that the process was neutral and objective, but in reality, it was influenced by politics, racism, and economic interests.

Another ideological effect comes from who created the data. Since it was collected by government officials, it only shows one side‚Äîthe state‚Äôs perspective‚Äîand not the voices of the people who were deported. This imbalance shows how data can reflect the power structure of its time. In short, the dataset not only documents deportations but also carries the ideas and values of authority and control that shaped U.S. immigration policy back then.
