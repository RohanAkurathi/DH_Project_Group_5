Key Events

First Period: 1900–1907

Foundations of Modern Immigration Enforcement

English Immigration c. 1880

Growing Popularity of Steamships

The spread of steam-powered ships replaced sail ships, dramatically reducing travel times and costs for English immigrants. This technological advancement substantially reduced the difficulty of transatlantic migration.

Impact: Cheaper and faster ocean crossings opened immigration to working-class English families who previously could not afford the journey.

Federal Law 1882

Chinese Exclusion Act

This landmark legislation reduced immigration and labor supply from China. The resulting labor shortages created a new demand for cheap labor from Mexico to replace Chinese workers.

Key Effects

  • Suspended immigration of Chinese laborers for 10 years
  • Barred Chinese from naturalization
  • Created labor vacuum in railroad construction and agriculture
  • Mexican workers increasingly recruited to fill these positions

Legacy: This was the first significant law restricting immigration based on nationality, setting a precedent for future exclusionary policies.

Mexican Immigration 1884

Completion of the Mexican Central Railroad

Connected the U.S. and Mexican rail networks, making cross-border migration significantly easier and cheaper, facilitating sustained flows of Mexican labor northward.

Impact on Migration

  • Direct rail connections from central Mexico to U.S. border
  • Labor recruiters (enganchadores) could travel deep into Mexico's interior
  • Workers from Guanajuato, Jalisco, Michoacán, and Zacatecas now had access to U.S. jobs
  • El Paso became a major gateway for Mexican labor migration
Federal Law March 3, 1903

Anarchist Exclusion Act of 1903

Expanded U.S. immigration law to exclude certain categories of immigrants based on political beliefs and other characteristics. Passed in response to President McKinley's assassination by an anarchist.

Excludable Classes Added

  • Anarchists — those "opposed to all organized government"
  • Beggars
  • Importers of prostitutes
  • People with epilepsy

Deportation Provisions

  • Allowed deportation within two years for immigrants who entered unlawfully
  • Deportation for those who became a "public charge"
  • First federal law authorizing ideological deportation

Note: This act was the first to exclude immigrants based on political beliefs, establishing precedent for future ideological deportations during the Red Scare.

Second Period: 1907–1915

Restriction, Revolution, and the Roots of Mass Deportation

Federal Law February 20, 1907

Immigration Act of 1907

Passed by the 59th Congress and signed by President Theodore Roosevelt, this landmark legislation marked a turning point in American immigration restriction.

Deportation Provisions

  • First conduct-based deportation: The first law authorizing deportation for criminal behavior committed after arrival in the U.S.
  • Public charge clause: Aliens who became a "public charge" within three years of entry due to pre-existing conditions could be deported
  • Prostitution provisions: Foreign women engaged in prostitution or living in brothels within three years of entry faced deportation
  • Expanded "immoral purposes": Banned importing foreign women for "prostitution or any other immoral purpose"

Excludable Classes

  • Idiots, imbeciles, feeble-minded persons, epileptics, and the insane
  • Paupers, beggars, and those likely to become public charges
  • Persons with tuberculosis or contagious diseases
  • Those convicted of crimes involving moral turpitude
  • Polygamists and anarchists
  • Contract laborers

Federal Law March 2, 1907

Expatriation Act of 1907

A law that tied American women's citizenship to their marital status, with significant implications for deportation.

Key Provisions

  • American women who married foreign nationals automatically lost their U.S. citizenship
  • Loss of citizenship could lead to deportation and denial of re-entry
  • During WWI, women married to German men had to register as "enemy aliens"

Notable Women Affected

  • Harriot Stanton Blatch — Suffrage movement leader
  • Inez Milholland — Attorney and suffragist
  • Crystal Eastman — Attorney and feminist

Repeal: Partially overturned by the Cable Act of 1922, though women married to "aliens ineligible for citizenship" (i.e., Asian men) remained affected until 1931.

Mexican Immigration 1907

Mexican Economic Crisis & Repatriation

Mexico suffered a severe economic downturn in 1906, sliding into a full depression by 1907–1909.

Context

  • 1906: Mexico experienced a sharp economic recession
  • 1907–1909: The depression deepened
  • Immigration to the U.S. increased as nativist sentiment grew in the Southwest

1907 Repatriation: The Mexican government allocated funds to repatriate some Mexican nationals living in the United States during the economic crisis — an early precedent for later mass deportations.

Mexican Immigration November 20, 1910

The Mexican Revolution Begins

Francisco I. Madero launched an armed uprising against the dictator Porfirio Díaz.

Background

  • Plan of San Luis Potosí: Madero called for revolution on November 20, 1910
  • Díaz had ruled for 35 years under an authoritarian regime
  • Land concentration and stark inequality fueled widespread discontent

Impact on Migration

  • War refugees began migrating northward in large numbers
  • Between 1910 and 1920, an estimated 1–1.5 million Mexicans entered the U.S.
  • Mexican-origin populations surged in Texas and California

Government Report 1911

The Dillingham Commission Report

After more than three years and over $1 million in expenses, this massive immigration study was released.

Commission Composition

  • Chairman: Senator William P. Dillingham (R-Vermont)
  • 3 senators, 3 representatives, and 3 presidential appointees as "experts"
  • Employed over 300 staff members

The Report (41 Volumes)

  • Statistical analyses of immigrants' occupations, living conditions, education, legislation, and social organizations
  • Volume 5: Daniel Folkmar's "Dictionary of Races or Peoples"
  • Franz Boas's study on physical changes in immigrant children

Key Conclusions & Recommendations

  • Southern and Eastern European immigrants posed a "serious threat" to American society
  • Implement literacy tests
  • Continue Chinese exclusion; restrict Japanese and Korean immigration
  • Limit unskilled foreign workers
  • Establish a quota system

Legacy: Despite data showing immigrants' overall positive contributions, the Commission's restrictionist recommendations won out—paving the way for the 1917 Literacy Test Act and the 1921 Quota Act.

International Event August 1914

World War I Breaks Out

War erupted in Europe, with far-reaching consequences for U.S. immigration policy.

Effects on Immigration

  • European immigration dropped sharply
  • Angel Island actually saw an increase in non-Asian immigrants
  • Set the stage for the Immigration Act of 1917
  • The Bureau of Investigation began investigating draft evaders and suspected spies

Third Period: 1916–1921

War, Waivers, and the Origins of Mass Deportation

Federal Law February 5, 1917

Immigration Act of 1917 (Asiatic Barred Zone Act)

A comprehensive immigration restriction law affecting immigrants from Asia and Pacific regions.

Key Provisions

  • Literacy tests required for those over 16 years old
  • Created the "Asiatic Barred Zone" prohibiting immigration from most of Asia
  • Increased head taxes on immigrants
  • Expanded list of excludable classes

Impact: This act represented the culmination of decades of restrictionist efforts and the Dillingham Commission's recommendations.

Mexican Immigration 1917–1918

Immigration Act of 1917 Waivers for Mexicans

Mexicans were given waivers to account for labor shortages during World War I, creating exceptions to the new restrictions.

Exemptions Granted

  • Waived the head tax for Mexican workers
  • Exempted from contract labor provisions
  • Waived literacy test requirements

Significance: These waivers established a pattern of treating Mexican labor as a flexible workforce — welcomed during shortages, targeted during downturns.

Mexican Immigration 1920–1921

Economic Depression of 1920–1921

During this economic downturn, Mexican workers were deported in order to provide relief for social welfare agencies.

Context

  • Post-WWI recession caused widespread unemployment
  • Mexican workers scapegoated as economic burden
  • Deportations used to reduce welfare agency costs
  • Pattern of economic-driven deportation established

Foreshadowing: The majority of these types of deportations would happen later in the 1930s during the Great Depression, when between 500,000 and 2 million Mexicans and Mexican Americans were forcibly removed.
